Eastshade

Joshua J. Daymude
February 7, 2021 2 min read videogame-review

The opening hour of Eastshade charades as yet another open-world role-playing game with bad physics and fetch quests but the hope for a decent, rustic-medieval-fantasy story. “From these humble beginnings, I’ll make my heroic mark on this world,” you say to yourself as you arrive in the game’s charming but somewhat prototypical starting village. But Eastshade, dear reader, is not a fantasy island through which to blaze a fiery campaign of blood and magic; rather, it asks you to find joy in drinking herbal teas and riding a penny-farthing through the forest to find the perfect vista to capture on your canvas. As a professional painter in a fully nonviolent world, an easel and brush replace sword and sorcery as you quest to capture your late mother’s favorite sights on the island.

In theory, an experience emphasizing non-violence and exploring role-playing-as-a-painter should have been a fresh take that I’d fall for. Instead, for a game that uses literal inspiration as currency for your character’s painting capabilities, I completed Eastshade feeling markedly uninspired. Freedom from the shackles of combat-oriented game design could have opened entirely new directions for innovative play, but Eastshade feels like it has a combat-shaped hole someone forgot to fill.

Emphasizing relationships and conversation could have played out as intricate, interconnected social drama to immerse in, but interactinos in Eastshade primarily drives fetch quests that only occasionally involve painting, the game’s crowning mechanic. And — while perhaps I’m just picky — a game about painting should have tried a little harder on graphics and art.

It’s basically phototourism with the occasionally intriguing conversation.

Eastshade is an open-world role-playing game that casts the player as a professional painter visiting their late mother’s favorite island getaway. In a quest to honor your mother’s memory by painting her favorite places in Eastshade, you meet and help a reasonably sized cast of the island’s anthropomorphic animal inhabitants. Some want you to paint their favorite things, some need to get out of debt, and some just want a friend. In theory, an experience that emphasizes non-violence and explores role-playing-as-a-painter should have been a fresh and interesting take I’d have fallen for. Instead, for a game that uses literal inspiration as currency for your character’s painting capabilities, I was left feeling markedly uninspired.
